Why does Spectrum Reach get buried by traditional media — when its multiscreen product is more targeted?

The honest story · Traditional vs multiscreen advertising in the Reno DMA · Spectrum Reach MR&I · 2026-06-18 · public sources only · national benchmarks + Reno-verified demographics

You asked a fair question with a built-in thesis: Spectrum Reach offers the superior (targeted, multiscreen) solution, but it's getting buried by traditional broadcast — is that an education gap, and does an aging population explain it? The data mostly supports your instincts, but corrects two of them. The short version: Spectrum isn't simply losing to broadcast because buyers are ignorant. It's being squeezed from two sides, the people who sign the checks really do skew old (just not the Reno population), and "superior" doesn't automatically win when the better product is harder to understand and more expensive per impression.

The honest answer, up front

  1. "Buried by broadcast" is only half true. At the local level, digital has already passed traditional (52% vs 48% of 2026 local ad spend). The bigger gravitational pull on small-business dollars isn't broadcast — it's cheap, self-serve digital (Facebook is used by ~83% of SMBs). Broadcast is the incumbent that holds the TV budget; DIY digital is what's eating the growth.
  2. But within TV dollars, linear/broadcast genuinely still dominates (~81% of local TV-set spend in 2026). So your read on broadcast's grip on television money is correct — and it persists for real reasons (trust, reach, low CPM, simplicity), not pure inertia.
  3. The targeting-efficiency advantage is real. Linear wastes a lot: ~94% of linear impressions reach only ~55% of viewers. Multiscreen/CTV targets to the ZIP or household and optimizes in real time. But it costs more per impression and is harder to measure — so the advantage has to be sold and proven, not assumed.
  4. The age friction is real — but it's the business owners, not Reno. Reno's population median age is 38.9, essentially the US average. The aging that matters is the decision-makers: ~52% of US business owners are 55+. The people buying ads grew up on broadcast.
  5. So why is Spectrum buried? A conservative, older buyer + a real education/measurement gap + linear's legitimate strengths + a squeeze between trusted broadcast above and cheap DIY digital below. The fix is education-led, proof-driven, simplified selling — not a better mousetrap.

Your instinct is right Targeted multiscreen genuinely reduces wasted spend — you don't pay to reach people outside the client's geography or demo, and you can optimize mid-flight. For a niche advertiser (a single dealership, a med-spa, a law firm), that efficiency is a decisive advantage.

But the honest caveat: linear isn't irrational. Local broadcast carries the highest consumer trust of any medium (viewers are ~4× more likely to trust brands advertising locally), delivers near-universal mass reach, is non-skippable, and is cheaper per impression. For mass-market local categories — casinos, auto, healthcare systems wanting everyone in the DMA — broad reach is a feature, not a bug. The industry's own conclusion is that broadcast + streaming work best together; streaming adds precision and frequency, broadcast supplies reach. (StreamingMedia "Local Broadcast Advantage"; TVB 2025 — note TVB is a broadcast trade body, treat as directional.) Source bias

Implication Spectrum shouldn't pitch multiscreen as "the replacement for broadcast." It should pitch "broadcast's reach + streaming's precision in one buy, with the waste taken out and the results measured." That reframes the more expensive CPM as less wasted spend, which is exactly your efficiency argument.

3 · Is there an education gap? Yes — and it's the real openingWhy "more targeted" doesn't translate into adoption

Fact The documented barriers to CTV/multiscreen adoption among local advertisers are complexity/fragmentation (dozens of platforms — Roku, Hulu, Samsung, YouTube TV…), measurement confusion (no single standard for proving reach/ROI), and plain awareness/misconceptions. (JamLoop; eMarketer CTV FAQ 2026.)

Fact Adoption is nonetheless rising: 40%+ of small businesses have used or plan to use CTV within a year, pulled in by low self-serve minimums (~$500 on Hulu/Roku). (Adwave CTV stats 2025.)

So the education gap cuts two ways. It explains why a superior product lags — buyers don't understand or can't measure it. Inference But it's also precisely Spectrum Reach's white space: the very things SMBs find hard (aggregating platforms, targeting, measurement) are what an operator with first-party data and managed service can do for them. The competitor that teaches the Reno market wins it. Education isn't overhead here — it's the go-to-market.

5 · So why is Spectrum Reach getting buried? The two-sided squeezePutting it together honestly

Spectrum's premium, managed, multiscreen offering sits in the middle of a market that pulls budgets to two easier extremes:

↑ Pulled UP to traditional broadcast — trusted, mass-reach, cheap CPM, one simple buy, an existing rep relationship, and a 55+ owner who already believes in it. (KOLO/Gray, KTVN/Sarkes, KRNV-KRXI/Sinclair, radio.)
SPECTRUM REACH sits here: premium multiscreen — more targeted & measurable, but pricier per impression, harder to understand, and requiring a salesperson to explain.
↓ Pulled DOWN to cheap DIY digital — Meta/Google/Hulu self-serve from ~$500, no salesperson, instant, "good enough," and where SMB budgets already concentrate. (Facebook ~83% of SMBs.)

The synthesis "Superior" loses to "familiar" (broadcast) and to "cheap & easy" (DIY digital) whenever the superior option is harder to understand, harder to measure, and more expensive up front. That's not a product failure — it's a communication, proof, and friction failure. Every structural fact above points to the same fix.

6 · What this means for the GTM refresh

  • Lead with education, not inventory. The buyer doesn't understand multiscreen/measurement; the seller who teaches the market owns it. Make "how targeted TV actually works" the opening, not the rate card.
  • Reframe price as waste removed. Use the 94%→55% linear-waste stat: a higher CPM that reaches only the client's real customers can be cheaper per actual prospect than cheap-but-wasteful linear. This is your efficiency argument, made sellable.
  • Sell "broadcast + streaming," not "instead of." Meet the trust/reach belief head-on — add precision and measurement on top of the reach owners already value. Avoid making it a referendum on broadcast.
  • Productize the hard parts. Aggregation across platforms + first-party targeting + one measurement report is exactly what SMBs find too complex to DIY. That's the managed-service moat against both broadcast and self-serve.
  • Match the consultative pitch to an older buyer. A 55+ owner wants a trusted advisor who handles complexity — not a self-serve login. Spectrum's human, local sales model is an asset here, if it's framed as guidance.
  • Prove it locally. The fastest cure for the education gap is a measured Reno case study (a dealer, a casino, a health system) showing targeted multiscreen beating a linear-only or DIY-only baseline. Nothing closes the gap like local proof.
